## 1. INTRODUCTION TO BATTERIES

### What is a Battery?

-   **Definition**: Devices that store energy in chemical form and
    convert it to electrical energy when required
-   **Principle**: Based on **redox reactions** (reduction-oxidation)
    and electron transfer
-   **Key characteristic**: Oxidation and reduction reactions are
    **physically separated**, allowing insertion of a load between them
-   Electrochemical potential difference = **voltage**, electron
    exchange = **current**

### Applications of Batteries

-   **Consumer electronics**: Watches, remote controls, cameras,
    laptops, mobile phones, toys
-   **Automotive**: Car starters (high current for short duration)
-   **Medical**: Cardiac pacemakers (small, steady current, long
    duration)
-   **Industrial**: UPS systems, backup power, submarines, torpedoes
-   **Specialized**: Hearing aids (tiny), military applications
    (rechargeable, high power)

## 2. ELECTROCHEMICAL CELLS

### Components of an Electrochemical Cell

1.  **Electrodes** (Two):

    -   **Anode**: Site of oxidation (loses electrons)
    -   **Cathode**: Site of reduction (gains electrons)

2.  **Electrolyte**: Ionic conductor that allows charged ions to move
    between electrodes

3.  **Separator**: Porous, electronically insulating material between
    anode and cathode

4.  **Current Collectors**: Thin foils of aluminum or copper for
    efficient electron transfer

### Types of Electrochemical Cells

#### A. Galvanic Cells

-   **Function**: Produce electricity from spontaneous redox reactions
-   **Characteristics**:
    -   Anode is negative terminal
    -   Cathode is positive terminal
    -   Converts chemical → electrical energy
    -   Two electrodes in one or two electrolytes (connected by salt
        bridge if different)
-   **Example**: Daniel Cell
    -   **Anode**: Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻ (oxidation)
    -   **Cathode**: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu (reduction)
    -   **Overall**: Zn + Cu²⁺ → Zn²⁺ + Cu

#### B. Electrolytic Cells

-   **Function**: Non-spontaneous reactions driven by external current
-   **Characteristics**:
    -   Anode is positive terminal
    -   Cathode is negative terminal
    -   Converts electrical → chemical energy
    -   Single electrolyte, two electrodes
-   **Applications**: Electroplating, electrorefining, metal
    purification

### Cell Representation Convention

-   Anode on **left**, cathode on **right**
-   Vertical line (\|) or semicolon separates electrode from electrolyte
-   Double vertical lines (\|\|) indicate salt bridge
-   **Example**: Zn\|ZnSO₄ (1M) \|\| CuSO₄(1M)\|Cu

### Anode vs Cathode

  Feature             Anode                    Cathode
  ------------------- ------------------------ -------------------------
  Reaction            Oxidation occurs         Reduction occurs
  Electrons           Produced                 Consumed
  Ion migration       Anions migrate towards   Cations migrate towards
  Galvanic cell       Negative (−)             Positive (+)
  Electrolytic cell   Positive (+)             Negative (−)

## 3. TYPES OF BATTERIES

### A. Primary Batteries (Non-rechargeable)

-   **Definition**: Galvanic cells that cannot be efficiently recharged
-   **Characteristics**:
    -   Chemicals sealed in at manufacture
    -   Cell reaction cannot be reversed
    -   Must be discarded when "dead" (reaction reaches equilibrium)
    -   Also called "throw-away" or "irreversible" batteries
-   **Requirements**:
    -   Compact, lightweight
    -   Economical, environmentally benign
    -   High energy density, long shelf life
    -   Constant voltage, long discharge period
-   **Examples**: Dry cell, Lithium-copper sulfide cell

### B. Secondary Batteries (Rechargeable)

-   **Definition**: Cells rechargeable by passing current through them
-   **Mechanism**: External source reverses spontaneous cell reaction,
    restoring reactants
-   **Characteristics**:
    -   Large number of charge-discharge cycles
    -   Also called "storage cells" or "accumulators"
-   **Requirements**:
    -   Long shelf-life (charged and discharged states)
    -   Long cycle life and design life
    -   High power-to-weight ratio
    -   Short recharge time
    -   High voltage, high energy density
-   **Examples**: Lead-acid, Nickel-cadmium (NiCd), Nickel-metal hydride
    (NiMH), Lithium-ion (Li-ion)

## 4. BATTERY PERFORMANCE METRICS

### a. Current

-   **Definition**: Rate of flow of electrons during discharge
-   **Units**: Amperes (A) = Coulombs/second
-   **Requirement**: High conductance electrolyte for uniform current

### b. Capacity

-   **Definition**: Charge in ampere-hours (Ah) obtainable from battery
-   **Determined by**: Mass of active materials consumed during
    discharge
-   **Formula**: C = (m × n × F) / M
    -   C = capacity (Ah)
    -   m = mass of active material
    -   n = number of electrons
    -   F = Faraday constant (96,500 C)
    -   M = molar mass
-   **Measurement**: Time (t) to reach minimum voltage at fixed
    discharge current
-   **Indicator**: Longer flat portion of voltage-time curve = better
    capacity

### c. Energy Efficiency

-   **Formula**: (Energy released on discharge / Energy required for
    charging) × 100%
-   **Factors affecting**:
    -   Current efficiency of electrode processes
    -   Overpotentials during discharge/charge
    -   Internal resistance
-   **Requirement**: High energy efficiency

### d. Cycle Life

-   **Definition**: Number of charge-discharge cycles before failure
-   **Applies to**: Secondary batteries only
-   **Factors affecting**:
    -   Chemical composition
    -   Morphological changes
    -   Corrosion at contact points
    -   Shedding of active material
    -   Short circuits from irregular crystal growth

### e. Shelf Life

-   **Definition**: Storage period under specified conditions while
    retaining performance
-   **Affected by**: Self-discharge (reaction between anode/cathode
    materials or corrosion)

### f. Energy Density

-   **Definition**: Ratio of energy available to battery mass or volume
-   **Units**: Wh/kg (gravimetric) or Wh/L (volumetric)
-   **Measurement**: Capacity × average voltage / total mass or volume

### g. Power Density

-   **Definition**: Ratio of power available to battery mass or volume
-   **Units**: W/kg or W/L
-   **Requirement**: Continuous high power density or high value for
    short periods

## 5. ELECTROMOTIVE FORCE (EMF) AND ELECTROCHEMISTRY

### Electromotive Force (EMF)

-   **Definition**: Potential difference causing current flow from
    higher to lower potential
-   **Symbol**: E_cell (volts, V)
-   **Formula**: E_cell = E_cathode − E_anode

### Standard EMF

-   **Conditions**:
    -   Unit concentration/activity of reactants and products
    -   Temperature = 298 K
    -   Pressure = 1 atm
    -   Pure solids and liquids
-   **Symbol**: E°\_cell

### Thermodynamic Relation

-   **Formula**: ΔG = −nFE
    -   ΔG = free energy change
    -   n = number of electrons transferred
    -   F = Faraday constant
    -   E = EMF
-   **Spontaneity**: Reaction is spontaneous when ΔG \< 0, which
    requires E \> 0

### Standard Electrode Potential (E°)

-   **Definition**: Electrode potential at unit concentration, 298 K, 1
    atm pressure
-   **Reference**: Standard hydrogen electrode (SHE) = 0 V
-   **Electrochemical Series**: Elements arranged by increasing standard
    electrode potential

### Selected Standard Electrode Potentials

  Element     Half Reaction     E° (V)
  ----------- ----------------- --------
  Lithium     Li⁺ + e⁻ → Li     −3.03
  Potassium   K⁺ + e⁻ → K       −2.92
  Aluminum    Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al   −1.66
  Zinc        Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Zn   −0.76
  Iron        Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Fe   −0.44
  Lead        Pb²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Pb   −0.13
  Hydrogen    2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂    0.00
  Copper      Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu   +0.34
  Silver      Ag⁺ + e⁻ → Ag     +0.80
  Gold        Au³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Au   +1.52

### Nernst Equation

**For electrode potential:** - E = E° − (2.303RT/nF) log\[Mⁿ⁺\] - At 298
K: E = E° − (0.0592/n) log\[Mⁿ⁺\]

**For cell EMF:** - E_cell = E°\_cell − (2.303RT/nF) log(\[C\]^c\[D\]^d
/ \[A\]^a\[B\]^b) - At 298 K: E_cell = E°\_cell − (0.0591/n)
log(\[C\]^c\[D\]^d / \[A\]^a\[B\]^b)

**Significance:** 1. Calculate cell potential under non-standard
conditions 2. Measure equilibrium constant (when E = 0) 3. Compute Gibbs
free energy 4. Predict reaction spontaneity

## 6. ELECTROLYTES

### Types of Electrolytes

1.  **Liquid Electrolytes**:
    -   Low viscosity
    -   High energy density
    -   High charge-discharge rate capability
    -   Operating temperature: −40°C to 60°C
    -   Low flammability (but some are flammable)
2.  **Solid Polymeric Electrolytes**:
    -   High flexibility
    -   High energy density
    -   Good safety and mechanical properties
    -   Thermal and chemical stability
    -   **Limitation**: Low ionic conductivity at room temperature (10⁻⁵
        to 10⁻¹ mS/cm)
3.  **Gel Polymeric Electrolytes**:
    -   High ionic conductivity (\~1 mS/cm)
    -   High flexibility
    -   Chemical stability
    -   **Limitations**: Poor mechanical strength, poor interfacial
        properties

### Ideal Electrolyte Properties

-   High ionic conductivity
-   Zero electronic conductivity
-   Non-reactive with electrode materials
-   Wide operating temperature range

### Advantages of Solid Polymeric Electrolytes

-   No electrolyte leakage
-   High safety (non-flammable, non-volatile)
-   Thermal and mechanical stability
-   Easy fabrication
-   High power density and cyclability

## 7. LEAD-ACID BATTERY

### Construction

-   **Anode grid**: Filled with finely divided spongy lead (Pb)
-   **Cathode grid**: Packed with lead dioxide (PbO₂)
-   **Electrolyte**: Sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) solution, specific gravity
    \~1.25
-   **Separator**: Strips of wood, rubber, or glass fiber between grids
-   **Container**: Plastic or hard vulcanized rubber
-   **Type**: Wet cell (aqueous electrolyte)
-   **Cell voltage**: \~2.1 V per cell

### Reactions

**During Discharge:** - **Anode**: Pb + SO₄²⁻ → PbSO₄ + 2e⁻ -
**Cathode**: PbO₂ + 4H⁺ + SO₄²⁻ + 2e⁻ → PbSO₄ + 2H₂O - **Overall**: Pb +
PbO₂ + 2H₂SO₄ → 2PbSO₄ + 2H₂O

**During Charging:** - **Cathode**: PbSO₄ + 2e⁻ → Pb + SO₄²⁻ -
**Anode**: PbSO₄ + 2H₂O → PbO₂ + SO₄²⁻ + 4H⁺ + 2e⁻ - **Overall**:
2PbSO₄ + 2H₂O → Pb + PbO₂ + 2H₂SO₄

### Overcharging - Electrolysis of Water

-   **Reaction**: 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂
-   **Consequences**:
    -   Reduction in acid level
    -   Damage to exposed electrode grids
    -   Risk of explosion (pressure build-up)
-   **Prevention**:
    -   Use Pb-Ca alloy anode (inhibits water electrolysis)
    -   Catalyst (98% CeO₂ + 2% Pt) recombines H₂ and O₂
    -   Maintenance-free sealed batteries

### Applications

1.  **Automotive**: SLI (Starting, Lighting, Ignition) in cars and
    trucks
2.  **Industrial**: Electric trucks, submarines, mine locomotives
3.  **Stationary Backup**: Telecommunication, hospitals, railway signals
4.  **Consumer**: Emergency lighting, security systems, UPS, power tools

### Advantages

-   High efficiency (\~80% voltage efficiency)
-   Long service life (300-1500 recharges; sealed types: 2000+)
-   Relatively fast recharge (2-8 hours)
-   Low self-discharge
-   High current capability (\>10 A)
-   Reversible operation

### Disadvantages

-   **Sulfation**: Large PbSO₄ crystals form if left partially charged
    (ruins battery)
-   **Low energy density**: \~35 Wh/kg (heavy weight)
-   **Voltage decreases** with H₂SO₄ concentration drop
-   **Temperature sensitive**: Decreased efficiency at low temperatures
-   **Corrosive electrolyte**: H₂SO₄ is hazardous
-   **Toxic lead**: Environmental health problems
-   **Grid corrosion**: Primary failure cause

## 8. NICKEL-METAL HYDRIDE (NiMH) BATTERY

### Construction

-   **Anode**: Nickel oxyhydroxide (NiOOH)
-   **Cathode**: Hydrogen-absorbing alloys
-   **Separator**: Fine fibers
-   **Electrolyte**: Aqueous solution (mainly KOH)
-   **Container**: Metal case with self-resealing safety vent
-   **Structure**: Similar to Ni-Cd batteries

### Hydrogen-Absorbing Alloys

-   **Types**:
    -   AB (TiFe)
    -   AB₂ (ZnMn₂)
    -   AB₅ (LaNi₅) - preferred for batteries
    -   A₂B (Mg₂Ni)
-   **Capacity**: Absorb hydrogen \~1000 times their volume
-   **Mechanism**: Combine metal A (exothermic hydride) with metal B
    (endothermic hydride)

### Working Principle

-   **Key characteristic**: Hydrogen moves between electrodes;
    electrolyte does not participate
-   **Charge**: H moves from positive to negative electrode
-   **Discharge**: H moves from negative to positive electrode
-   **Result**: No change in electrolyte volume

### Reactions

-   **Positive electrode**: NiOOH + H₂O + e⁻ ⇌ Ni(OH)₂ + OH⁻
-   **Negative electrode**: H + OH⁻ ⇌ H₂O + e⁻
-   **Overall**: Hydrogen transfer between electrodes

### Advantages

1.  **High energy density**: \~2× that of Ni-Cd batteries
2.  **High capacity**: 1000-3000 mAh or more
3.  **Long cycle life**: Hundreds to thousands of cycles
4.  **Environmentally friendly**: No toxic cadmium
5.  **Enhanced safety**: Stable, low thermal runaway risk
6.  **Cost-effective**: Rechargeable many times
7.  **Similar voltage to Ni-Cd**: Easy replacement

### Disadvantages

1.  **High self-discharge**: 1-5% per day when idle
2.  **Memory effect**: Less prone than Ni-Cd but still present
3.  **Voltage sag**: Under heavy loads
4.  **Temperature sensitive**: Poor performance at extremes
5.  **Slow charging**: Limited fast-charge capability
6.  **Lower voltage output**: vs newer chemistries

### Applications

-   Consumer electronics
-   Power tools
-   Medical devices
-   Hybrid vehicles
-   Emergency lighting and backup power
-   Renewable energy storage
-   Flashlights and portable devices
-   Electric bicycles and scooters

## 9. LITHIUM-ION (Li-ION) BATTERY

### Construction

-   **Anode**: Lithium-graphite intercalate (LiₓC₆)
-   **Cathode**: Transition metal oxide (typically CoO₂; also MnO₂,
    NiO₂)
-   **Electrolyte**: Organic solvent (diethyl carbonate or propylene
    carbonate) with LiPF₆ or LiBF₄ salt
-   **Type**: Intercalation compounds at both electrodes
-   **Note**: No lithium metal present (hence "lithium-ion")

### Working Principle

**During Discharge:** - **Anode**: Li-C → Li⁺ + e⁻ + C - **Cathode**:
Li⁺ + e⁻ + CoO₂ → LiCoO₂ - **Overall**: CoO₂ + Li-C → LiCoO₂ + C - **Ion
movement**: Li⁺ migrates from anode to cathode

**During Charge:** - Process reverses - Li⁺ migrates back into
graphite - Cobalt oxidizes from Co³⁺ to Co⁴⁺

### Advantages

1.  **High energy density**: \~150 Wh/kg (vs 25 Wh/kg for lead-acid)
2.  **Long cycle life**: 400-1200 cycles
3.  **Lightweight**: Smaller and lighter than Ni-Cd or NiMH
4.  **High voltage**: \~3.7 V per cell (equivalent to 3 Ni-Cd cells)
5.  **Wide operating temperature range**
6.  **No memory effect**: Can be recharged before fully discharged
7.  **In-device charging**: No external charger needed

### Limitations

1.  **Poor charge retention**: Self-discharge \~10% per month
2.  **High cost**: Expensive materials and manufacturing
3.  **Safety concerns**: Flammable liquid electrolyte
4.  **Thermal runaway risk**: Can lead to fires/explosions
5.  **Aging**: Degrades over time even without use

### Liquid Electrolyte Limitations

1.  **Flammability**: Organic solvents ignite easily during thermal
    runaway
2.  **Volatility**: Evaporate at high temperatures
3.  **Limited temperature range**: Freeze at low temps, decompose at
    high temps

## 10. SAFETY CONCERNS WITH Li-ION BATTERIES

### Major Safety Hazards

#### a. Internal Shorts and Thermal Runaway

-   **Cause**: Damaged or malfunctioning cells
-   **Process**: Fast heating → chain reaction → thermal runaway
-   **Result**: Electrolyte decomposition → flammable gas emission →
    pressure buildup → venting/fire/explosion

#### b. Overcharging and Overheating

-   **Overcharging**: Excessive heat accumulation
-   **High temperature exposure**: Leaving device in hot car
-   **Result**: Thermal runaway

#### c. Physical Damage

-   **Cause**: Dropping, crushing battery
-   **Result**: Internal component damage → short circuits → thermal
    runaway

#### d. Manufacturing Defects

-   **Impact**: Increased thermal runaway risk
-   **Prevention**: Quality control procedures

#### e. Puncture or Penetration

-   **Result**: Internal shorts → thermal runaway
-   **Requirement**: Careful handling and proper disposal

#### f. Age and Degradation

-   **Effect**: Decreased capacity and performance over time
-   **Risk**: Increased susceptibility to overheating
-   **Prevention**: Avoid extreme discharges and temperature exposure

### Safety Measures

1.  **Battery Management Systems (BMS)**:
    -   Monitor and control charging/discharging
    -   Prevent overcharging, over-discharging, overheating
2.  **Thermal Management Systems**:
    -   Heat sinks and cooling fans
    -   Dissipate heat to prevent thermal runaway
3.  **Quality Control and Testing**:
    -   Detect defects before market release
4.  **Safety Standards and Regulations**:
    -   Government and industry safety requirements

## 11. EMERGING BATTERY TECHNOLOGIES

### a. Solid-State Batteries

-   **Innovation**: Solid electrolyte replaces liquid/gel electrolyte
-   **Advantages**:
    -   Increased energy density
    -   Enhanced safety (non-flammable)
    -   Potentially longer lifespan
-   **Applications**: Electric vehicles, portable devices

### b. Lithium-Sulfur (Li-S) Batteries

-   **Cathode**: Sulfur (abundant and cheap)
-   **Potential**: Greater energy density than Li-ion
-   **Challenges**:
    -   Low electrical conductivity of sulfur
    -   Dissolution of polysulfides

### c. Lithium-Air (Li-Air) Batteries

-   **Anode**: Lithium metal
-   **Cathode**: Oxygen from air
-   **Potential**: Extremely high theoretical energy density
    (\>gasoline)
-   **Challenges**:
    -   Limited cycle life
    -   Stability concerns

### d. Metal-Air Batteries

-   **Types**: Zinc-air, aluminum-air
-   **Anode**: Metal (Zn, Al)
-   **Cathode**: Oxygen from atmosphere
-   **Advantages**:
    -   High theoretical energy density
    -   Inexpensive (abundant materials)
-   **Challenges**: Reversibility, efficiency, cycle life

### e. Flow Batteries

-   **Principle**: Energy stored in liquid electrolytes in external
    tanks
-   **Types**: Vanadium redox flow batteries (VRFBs) most prominent
-   **Advantages**:
    -   Scalable energy storage
    -   Long lifespan
    -   Decoupled energy and power
-   **Applications**: Grid-scale energy storage

## 12. SODIUM-ION (Na-ION) BATTERIES

### Why Sodium?

**Lithium challenges:** - Relatively rare element - Limited geological
formations - Energy-intensive extraction - Cost fluctuations

**Sodium advantages:** - 6th most abundant element in Earth's crust -
Available in seawater and salt deposits - Simpler, less energy-intensive
extraction - More stable, potentially lower cost

### Chemical Similarities

-   Li and Na in same group (Group 1, Alkali Metals)
-   Similar chemical properties
-   Both lose single valence electron easily

### Key Difference: Ionic Radius

-   **Li⁺**: \~0.76 Å (smaller)
-   **Na⁺**: \~1.00 Å (larger)
-   **Impact**: Larger Na⁺ ions challenge electrode material design

### Challenges and Solutions

**Challenge**: Electrode materials must accommodate larger Na⁺ ions

**Solutions being explored:** - Prussian blue analogues - Layered sodium
vanadates - Novel electrode structures

**Current status:** - Lower energy and power density than Li-ion -
Ongoing optimization of materials and electrolytes

## 13. FUEL CELLS

### Definition and Principle

-   **Type**: Special galvanic cell
-   **Function**: Convert chemical energy in fuel-oxidant system →
    electrical energy
-   **Process**: Continuous electrochemical conversion
-   **Key difference from batteries**:
    -   Reactants supplied continuously from outside
    -   Products removed continuously
    -   Never "dead" as long as fuel supplied

### Components

-   Two electrodes
-   Electrolyte
-   Fuel (continuously supplied)
-   Oxidant (continuously supplied)

### Basic Reactions

-   **Anode**: Fuel + oxygen → oxidation products + ne⁻
-   **Cathode**: Oxidant + ne⁻ → reduction products

### Types of Fuel Cells

-   H₂-O₂ fuel cell
-   CH₃OH-O₂ fuel cell (Methanol fuel cell)
-   Solid-oxide fuel cell
-   Molten carbonate fuel cell
-   **Proton Exchange Membrane Fuel Cell (PEMFC)** - most common

## 14. PROTON EXCHANGE MEMBRANE FUEL CELL (PEMFC)

### Construction

-   **Electrolyte**: Ion-exchange polymeric membrane (solid polymer)
    -   Perfluorinated membrane (Nafion family)
    -   Excellent proton conductor
    -   Fluorocarbon polymer backbone + sulfonic acid groups
-   **Electrodes**: Porous carbon with Pt catalyst
-   **Fuel**: Hydrogen (H₂)
-   **Oxidant**: Oxygen/air (O₂)
-   **Operating temperature**: 40-80°C
-   **Charge carrier**: H⁺ (protons)

### Membrane Functions

1.  Acts as electrolyte (ionic communication anode ↔ cathode)
2.  Separates reactant gases
3.  Requires proper hydration for proton conductivity

### Reactions

-   **Anode**: H₂ → 2H⁺ + 2e⁻
-   **Cathode**: ½O₂ + 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂O
-   **Overall**: H₂ + ½O₂ → H₂O

### Performance

-   Operating temperature: \~80°C
-   Efficiency: \~60%
-   Power output: 5-200 kW
-   Lifetimes: \>60,000 hours at 80°C

### Advantages

1.  High power density
2.  Low weight and volume
3.  Fast startup (low operating temperature)
4.  Solid electrolyte (no leakage)
5.  No corrosive fluids
6.  Excellent gas crossover resistance
7.  High current densities (\>2 kW/L, 2 W/cm²)
8.  CO₂ tolerant

### Disadvantages

1.  Expensive platinum catalyst
2.  Sensitive to CO poisoning
3.  Narrow operating temperature range
4.  Difficult thermal management at high current densities
5.  Water management challenges (dehydration or flooding)
6.  High membrane cost

### Applications

-   Transportation (passenger vehicles, buses)
-   Stationary power generation
-   Portable power systems

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 15. METHANOL FUEL CELLS

### Construction

-   **Electrolyte**: Sulfuric acid
-   **Electrodes**: Porous carbon with Pt catalyst
-   **Fuel**: Methanol (CH₃OH)
-   **Oxidant**: Air
-   **Operating temperature**: 30-80°C
-   **Charge carrier**: H⁺

### Mechanism

1.  Electrosorption of methanol onto Pt layer (forms Pt-CH₂OH, Pt₂-CHOH
    species)
2.  Addition of oxygen to adsorbed carbon intermediates
3.  Generation of CO₂

### Advantages

-   Liquid fuel (easier handling than H₂)
-   Higher energy density than H₂ storage

### Disadvantages

-   Lower efficiency than H₂ fuel cells
-   Methanol crossover through membrane
-   CO₂ emissions (though lower than combustion)

### Advantages of Fuel Cells (General)

1.  **High efficiency**: 70-75% (vs 35-40% for thermal power plants)
2.  **No pollution**: No noise, chemical, or thermal pollution
3.  **Decentralized**: Can be located near point of use (no transmission
    losses \~30%)
4.  **Steady output**: Continuous as long as fuel supplied

## 17. ENVIRONMENTAL IMPACT OF BATTERY PRODUCTION

### a. Resource Extraction

-   **Materials**: Lithium, cobalt, nickel, graphite
-   **Impacts**:
    -   Habitat degradation
    -   Soil and water pollution
    -   Deforestation
    -   Displacement of indigenous communities
-   **Mitigation**: Responsible mining, material recycling and reuse

### b. Energy Intensity

-   **Process**: High energy consumption during electrode material
    fabrication and cell assembly
-   **Impact from fossil fuels**: Greenhouse gas emissions, air
    pollution
-   **Solution**: Use renewable energy (solar, wind) in manufacturing

### c. Chemical Pollution

-   **Chemicals used**: Solvents, electrolytes, coatings (may contain
    hazardous compounds)
-   **Risk**: Human health and environmental damage if improperly
    handled
-   **Mitigation**: Strict regulations, industry standards, safe
    handling and disposal

### d. Water Usage

-   **Uses**: Cooling, cleaning, chemical processing
-   **Impact**: Water resource strain, ecosystem effects
-   **Mitigation**: Water conservation measures, recycling

### e. End-of-Life Management

-   **Improper disposal**: Release of harmful chemicals into land and
    water
-   **Impact**: Pollution, ecosystem harm
-   **Solution**: Effective recycling infrastructure, material reuse

## 18. BATTERY RECYCLING AND SUSTAINABILITY

### Importance of Recycling

-   Minimize environmental impact
-   Recover valuable materials (lithium, cobalt, nickel)
-   Reduce need for new resource extraction
-   Prevent soil and water contamination

### Sustainable Practices

1.  **Responsible sourcing**: Ethical mining practices
2.  **Green manufacturing**: Renewable energy use, reduced emissions
3.  **Extended producer responsibility**: Manufacturer take-back
    programs
4.  **Consumer education**: Proper disposal and recycling
5.  **Circular economy**: Design for recyclability, material recovery
6.  **Regulatory frameworks**: Government policies and standards

## 20. KEY FORMULAS AND EQUATIONS

### Electrochemistry

1.  **EMF of cell**: E_cell = E_cathode − E_anode
2.  **Gibbs free energy**: ΔG = −nFE
3.  **Nernst equation (electrode)**: E = E° − (0.0592/n) log\[Mⁿ⁺\] (at
    298 K)
4.  **Nernst equation (cell)**: E_cell = E°\_cell − (0.0591/n) log(Q)
    (at 298 K)
5.  **Faraday constant**: F = 96,500 C/mol

### Battery Performance

6.  **Capacity**: C = (m × n × F) / M (Ah)
7.  **Energy efficiency**: η = (Energy discharged / Energy charged) ×
    100%
8.  **Energy density**: E_d = (Capacity × Voltage) / Mass (Wh/kg)
9.  **Power density**: P_d = Power / Mass (W/kg)

### Specific Battery Reactions

**Lead-Acid:** - Discharge: Pb + PbO₂ + 2H₂SO₄ → 2PbSO₄ + 2H₂O - Charge:
2PbSO₄ + 2H₂O → Pb + PbO₂ + 2H₂SO₄

**Li-ion:** - Discharge: CoO₂ + Li-C → LiCoO₂ + C - Charge: LiCoO₂ + C →
CoO₂ + Li-C

**PEMFC:** - Anode: H₂ → 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ - Cathode: ½O₂ + 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂O -
Overall: H₂ + ½O₂ → H₂O

## SUMMARY OF KEY CONCEPTS

1.  **Battery Fundamentals**: Redox reactions with physically separated
    oxidation and reduction, allowing load insertion

2.  **Primary vs Secondary**: Primary (non-rechargeable, single-use) vs
    Secondary (rechargeable, multiple cycles)

3.  **Performance Metrics**: Capacity, energy/power density, cycle life,
    shelf life, efficiency

4.  **Lead-Acid**: Heavy, low cost, automotive applications, sulfation
    problem

5.  **NiMH**: Better than Ni-Cd, environmentally friendly, high
    self-discharge

6.  **Li-ion**: High energy density, thermal runaway risk, dominant in
    portable electronics and EVs

7.  **Emerging Tech**: Solid-state (safer), Na-ion (abundant materials),
    Li-S/Li-air (ultra-high density potential)

8.  **Fuel Cells**: Continuous operation, high efficiency, clean (H₂O
    product), expensive catalysts

9.  **Environmental**: Resource extraction impacts, recycling crucial,
    sustainability focus

10. **Safety**: Li-ion thermal runaway, BMS systems, proper handling
    essential
